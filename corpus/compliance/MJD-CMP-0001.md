# BSA/AML Program Policy

## Purpose and Scope

This policy establishes the enterprise Bank Secrecy Act and Anti-Money Laundering (BSA/AML) program for Meridian John Doe Financial (Meridian J.D.), a synthetic fintech created for demonstration. It defines the governance, controls, and accountability that the institution maintains to detect, prevent, and report money laundering, terrorist financing, and other illicit finance activity across all products, channels, and legal entities.

The program applies to every business line, every employee, every officer, every member of the board, and every third party acting on behalf of Meridian J.D. It covers deposit accounts, payment products, card programs, lending, treasury, and any partner or banking-as-a-service relationship through which Meridian J.D. provides regulated financial services. No product may be launched and no customer relationship may be opened that falls outside the coverage of this program.

The four statutory pillars of the program, plus the fifth pillar added by the FinCEN Customer Due Diligence Rule, are addressed in this document and operationalized in the supporting procedures referenced in the Related Documents section. Those five pillars are: (1) a designated BSA Officer, (2) internal controls, (3) independent testing, (4) training, and (5) customer due diligence including beneficial ownership identification.

This policy is the parent control document for the compliance AML spine. Concrete operational thresholds, filing deadlines, and monitoring rules live in the child procedures and standards. Where this policy states a principle and a child document states a number, the child document governs the operational value and this policy governs the intent.

## Definitions

- **BSA Officer**: The individual designated by the board with authority, independence, and resources to administer the BSA/AML program day to day. At Meridian J.D. this role is held by the Chief Compliance Officer.
- **Money laundering**: The process of making illicitly obtained proceeds appear legitimate through placement, layering, and integration.
- **Terrorist financing**: The solicitation, collection, or provision of funds intended to support terrorism, regardless of whether the underlying funds are of legitimate origin.
- **Customer Due Diligence (CDD)**: The process of identifying a customer, understanding the nature and purpose of the relationship, and developing a customer risk profile.
- **Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)**: Heightened scrutiny applied to higher-risk customers and relationships, including more frequent review and senior approval.
- **Beneficial owner**: Each individual who owns, directly or indirectly, 25 percent or more of the equity interests of a legal entity customer, plus a single individual with significant managerial control (the control prong).
- **Suspicious Activity Report (SAR)**: The confidential FinCEN filing used to report known or suspected violations of law or suspicious transactions.
- **Currency Transaction Report (CTR)**: The FinCEN filing required for cash transactions that exceed the regulatory dollar threshold.
- **Politically Exposed Person (PEP)**: A current or former senior foreign political figure, their immediate family, and close associates. Treated as inherently higher risk.
- **Risk appetite**: The board-approved level of money-laundering and sanctions risk the institution is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives.
- **SAFE HARBOR**: The federal statutory protection under 31 U.S.C. 5318(g)(3) shielding the institution and its employees from civil liability for filing a SAR in good faith.
- **Placement, layering, integration**: The three classical stages of money laundering. Placement introduces illicit cash into the financial system, layering distances the funds from their source through complex movement, and integration returns the funds to the launderer as apparently legitimate wealth.
- **Money services business (MSB)**: A non-bank financial entity such as a money transmitter, currency dealer, or check casher. MSB customers are higher risk and require enhanced controls.
- **Correspondent account**: An account established to receive deposits from, make payments on behalf of, or handle other financial transactions for another financial institution. Foreign correspondent relationships carry heightened risk.
- **Structuring**: Deliberately breaking transactions into amounts below a reporting threshold to evade BSA reporting. Structuring is a federal crime regardless of the legality of the underlying funds.
- **Nested account**: A foreign financial institution's use of a Meridian J.D. correspondent account to gain indirect access to the U.S. financial system on behalf of its own customers, often without Meridian J.D.'s knowledge. A monitored risk.
- **314(a) request**: A FinCEN information request, issued under USA PATRIOT Act Section 314(a), asking financial institutions to search records for named subjects of significant money-laundering or terrorism investigations.
- **314(b) sharing**: Voluntary information sharing between financial institutions under a Section 314(b) safe harbor to identify and report potential money laundering or terrorist financing.

## 1. Program Governance

### 1.1 Board Oversight

1.1.1 The board of directors holds ultimate responsibility for the BSA/AML program. The board approves this policy on initial adoption and on every material revision, and at minimum annually.

1.1.2 The board receives, at least quarterly, a written BSA/AML report covering SAR filing volumes and trends, CTR volumes, open high-risk customer reviews, sanctions screening results, audit and examination findings, training completion rates, and the status of remediation for any control gaps.

1.1.3 The board documents its review of these reports in its minutes. Absence of documented board review is itself a reportable control deficiency under independent testing (section 4).

### 1.2 BSA Officer Designation

1.2.1 The board designates the BSA Officer by name and records the designation in the board minutes. The BSA Officer at Meridian J.D. is the Chief Compliance Officer.

1.2.2 The BSA Officer is vested with sufficient authority, autonomy, and resources to administer the program. The BSA Officer reports functionally to the board or a designated board committee and is not subordinate, for BSA purposes, to any revenue-generating line of business.

1.2.3 The BSA Officer may not be removed without board approval, and any removal or resignation is reported to the board with the reasons documented.

### 1.3 Three Lines of Defense

1.3.1 The first line of defense is the business and operations staff who execute customer-facing controls, including the Customer Identification Program, transaction processing, and frontline escalation of unusual activity.

1.3.2 The second line of defense is the Compliance function, which owns this policy, the monitoring rules, the SAR and CTR processes, sanctions screening governance, and independent challenge of first-line risk decisions.

1.3.3 The third line of defense is independent testing, performed by internal audit or a qualified external party, which validates the design and operating effectiveness of the entire program.

### 1.4 BSA/AML Steering Committee

1.4.1 A BSA/AML Steering Committee meets at least monthly, chaired by the BSA Officer and including representatives from Operations, Risk, Technology, Legal, and the relevant business lines. The committee reviews escalated cases, monitoring tuning proposals, emerging typologies, and the remediation status of audit and examination findings.

1.4.2 The committee maintains a documented charter, keeps minutes, and produces the quarterly board report described in section 1.1.2. Material decisions, including monitoring threshold changes and HIGH-risk relationship approvals beyond a defined exposure level, are recorded in the minutes with the supporting rationale.

1.4.3 Where the committee cannot reach consensus on a risk-acceptance decision, the matter is escalated to the BSA Officer, whose determination is final for BSA purposes and is reported to the board committee at the next quarterly cycle.

### 1.5 Resourcing and Independence

1.5.1 The BSA Officer prepares an annual resourcing assessment that maps program workload, including alert volume, case backlog, periodic review inventory, and filing volume, against staffing. Where workload exceeds capacity such that filing deadlines or review cadences are at risk, the BSA Officer escalates a resourcing request to the board committee.

1.5.2 Compliance staff performing second-line functions have no production, sales, or revenue targets that could compromise their independence. Compensation for BSA/AML staff is not tied to business-line revenue.

## 2. Risk Assessment

### 2.1 Enterprise BSA/AML Risk Assessment

2.1.1 Compliance maintains a documented, enterprise-wide BSA/AML risk assessment that is refreshed at least annually and upon any material change in products, customers, geographies, or channels.

2.1.2 The risk assessment scores inherent risk across the categories of products and services, customers and entities, geographies, and delivery channels. For each category it documents the inherent risk, the mitigating controls, and the residual risk.

2.1.3 The residual risk conclusions drive the calibration of the transaction monitoring thresholds defined in MJD-CMP-0004 and the EDD triggers defined in MJD-OPS-0003.

### 2.2 Customer Risk Rating

2.2.1 Every customer is assigned a risk rating of LOW, MODERATE, or HIGH at onboarding and is re-rated on the cadence in the table below or sooner upon a triggering event.

| Customer risk rating | Review cadence | Approval to open | Monitoring intensity |
|---|---|---|---|
| LOW | Every 36 months | Line staff | Standard automated rules |
| MODERATE | Every 24 months | Compliance analyst | Standard plus targeted rules |
| HIGH | Every 12 months | BSA Officer or delegate | Enhanced, with EDD per MJD-OPS-0003 |

2.2.2 Customers presenting one or more of the following attributes are rated HIGH by default: confirmed PEP status, cash-intensive business, money services business, foreign correspondent relationship, or nexus to a jurisdiction subject to comprehensive sanctions.

2.2.3 A downgrade from HIGH to MODERATE or LOW requires documented BSA Officer approval and a recorded rationale.

### 2.3 Risk Scoring Methodology

2.3.1 Inherent risk for each of the four risk-assessment categories is scored on a 1 to 5 scale, where 1 is low and 5 is high. A weighted composite produces the customer risk rating. The default category weights are products and services 30 percent, customers and entities 30 percent, geographies 25 percent, and delivery channels 15 percent.

2.3.2 The composite-to-rating mapping is fixed as follows, subject to the mandatory HIGH overrides in section 2.2.2 which take precedence over any composite score:

| Composite score | Customer risk rating |
|---|---|
| 1.0 to 2.3 | LOW |
| 2.4 to 3.6 | MODERATE |
| 3.7 to 5.0 | HIGH |

2.3.3 Any customer attribute listed in section 2.2.2 forces a HIGH rating regardless of the composite. Forced HIGH ratings cannot be overridden by the scoring model and may only be reduced through the documented downgrade process in section 2.2.3.

### 2.4 Product and Geographic Risk Factors

2.4.1 Products and services are assessed for inherent vulnerability to money laundering. Higher-risk products at Meridian J.D. include cross-border payments, prepaid and reloadable card programs, remote account opening, and banking-as-a-service partner programs that introduce indirect customers.

2.4.2 Geographic risk incorporates jurisdictions identified by the Financial Action Task Force as high-risk or subject to increased monitoring, jurisdictions subject to comprehensive sanctions (escalated under MJD-OPS-0008), and domestic High Intensity Drug Trafficking Areas and High Intensity Financial Crime Areas where relevant.

2.4.3 The geographic risk inputs are refreshed whenever an authoritative list changes and at minimum quarterly, and material changes are reported to the Steering Committee.

## 3. Internal Controls

### 3.1 Customer Identification and Due Diligence

3.1.1 The Customer Identification Program (CIP) is governed by MJD-OPS-0001 and collects, at minimum, name, date of birth, physical address, and a government identification number for each customer.

3.1.2 Customer Due Diligence is governed by MJD-OPS-0002 and establishes the nature and purpose of each relationship and the expected activity baseline used by monitoring.

3.1.3 Beneficial ownership is collected for every legal entity customer at the 25 percent ownership prong and the single control prong, consistent with the FinCEN CDD Rule.

### 3.2 Transaction Monitoring and Reporting

3.2.1 Automated transaction monitoring runs against all activity using the rules and thresholds in MJD-CMP-0004. Alerts are triaged, investigated, and dispositioned by Compliance.

3.2.2 Suspicious activity is reported via SAR using the procedure in MJD-CMP-0002. Reportable currency transactions are filed via CTR using the procedure in MJD-CMP-0003.

3.2.3 Sanctions screening against OFAC and applicable lists is governed by MJD-OPS-0008 and runs at onboarding and on an ongoing basis against the customer base and against every payment.

### 3.3 Recordkeeping

3.3.1 All BSA/AML records, including CIP records, CDD and EDD files, SAR and CTR filings and supporting documentation, and monitoring dispositions, are retained per MJD-CMP-0008.

3.3.2 SAR filings and the fact of their existence are confidential. Disclosure of a SAR or its contents outside the permitted channels is prohibited and is itself a federal violation.

### 3.4 Information Requests and Sharing

3.4.1 Meridian J.D. responds to FinCEN 314(a) requests within the timeframe specified in each request, searching current accounts, accounts closed within the preceding 12 months, and transactions conducted within the preceding 6 months for named subjects. A positive match is reported to FinCEN through the secure channel and never disclosed to the subject.

3.4.2 Meridian J.D. has filed the notice to participate in voluntary 314(b) information sharing. Sharing under 314(b) is limited to the statutory purpose, is logged, and is conducted only with other institutions that have filed the same notice.

3.4.3 The fact of a 314(a) match, like a SAR, is confidential and is restricted to the COMPLIANCE_OFFICER and RISK_ANALYST personas authorized for the AML spine.

### 3.5 Correspondent and Partner Program Controls

3.5.1 Foreign correspondent relationships and banking-as-a-service partner programs are subject to enhanced due diligence at onboarding and to ongoing oversight, including review of the partner's own AML program where the partner introduces indirect customers.

3.5.2 Nested-account risk is specifically assessed for correspondent relationships. Where a correspondent permits its customers to transact through the Meridian J.D. account, the relationship is rated HIGH and monitored accordingly.

3.5.3 Payable-through and shell-bank relationships are prohibited. Meridian J.D. does not maintain correspondent accounts for foreign shell banks and obtains certification that correspondents do not provide indirect access to shell banks.

## 4. Independent Testing

4.1 Independent testing of the BSA/AML program is performed at least every 12 months by internal audit or a qualified independent external party with no operational responsibility for the program.

4.2 The scope of independent testing includes the adequacy of the risk assessment, the effectiveness of internal controls, the timeliness and quality of SAR and CTR filings, the calibration of monitoring thresholds, the completeness of CDD and beneficial ownership collection, and training effectiveness.

4.3 Findings are rated by severity, assigned an owner and a remediation due date, and tracked to closure. The BSA Officer reports findings and remediation status to the board.

4.4 Independent-testing findings are remediated within service levels driven by severity: CRITICAL within 30 calendar days, HIGH within 60 calendar days, MODERATE within 90 calendar days, and LOW within 180 calendar days. Where a finding cannot be remediated within its service level, an interim compensating control and a revised due date are documented and approved by the BSA Officer.

4.5 Independent testing validates the integrity of the underlying data feeding monitoring and reporting, because a monitoring system that does not receive complete and accurate transaction data cannot detect suspicious activity regardless of rule quality. Data-completeness testing confirms that every in-scope product and channel feeds the monitoring system in MJD-CMP-0004.

## 5. Training

5.1 All employees complete BSA/AML training upon hire and at least annually thereafter. Role-specific training is delivered to staff in higher-risk functions, including frontline branch staff, operations, and compliance investigators.

5.2 Training completion is tracked. Failure to complete required training within the assigned window is escalated to the employee's manager and to the BSA Officer.

5.3 Board members receive BSA/AML training appropriate to their oversight role at least annually.

5.4 Training content is refreshed to reflect new typologies, regulatory changes, and lessons learned from internal cases and examinations. Role-specific modules include, at minimum: frontline identification of red flags and the no-tipping-off rule; operations handling of currency transactions feeding CTR detection (MJD-CMP-0003); and compliance investigator training on SAR narrative quality and deadline management (MJD-CMP-0002).

5.5 The institution maintains a documented red-flag catalog distributed in training, covering placement red flags (unusual cash patterns, structuring), layering red flags (rapid movement, pass-through activity), and integration red flags (funds returning as apparently legitimate income). The catalog aligns with the rule library in MJD-CMP-0004.

## 6. Sanctions and Office of Foreign Assets Control Linkage

6.1 Although sanctions compliance is operationalized in MJD-OPS-0008, the BSA/AML program treats sanctions screening as an integral control. The BSA Officer coordinates with the function responsible for OFAC screening so that customer risk ratings, monitoring, and sanctions screening share a consistent view of the customer and counterparty.

6.2 A confirmed sanctions match is blocked or rejected per MJD-OPS-0008 before any further processing, reported to OFAC within the required timeframe, and evaluated for whether the underlying activity also requires a SAR under MJD-CMP-0002. A single event can require both an OFAC blocking report and a SAR.

6.3 Sanctions list updates are ingested promptly and screening is rerun against the existing customer base when lists materially change, so that a newly listed party already on the books is identified.

## Roles and Responsibilities

- **Board of Directors**: Approves this policy, oversees the program, reviews quarterly reporting, and documents its review.
- **BSA Officer (Chief Compliance Officer)**: Administers the entire program, owns this policy, approves HIGH-risk relationships, and reports to the board.
- **Compliance Analysts (COMPLIANCE_OFFICER persona)**: Triage and investigate alerts, prepare SAR and CTR filings, conduct periodic reviews, and maintain the risk assessment.
- **Risk Analysts (RISK_ANALYST persona)**: Provide independent challenge, consume the risk assessment, and integrate AML risk into the enterprise risk framework.
- **First-line Operations and Branch Staff**: Execute CIP and CDD controls and escalate unusual activity.
- **Internal Audit / Independent Tester**: Performs the annual independent test.

## Exceptions and Escalation

Any exception to this policy must be requested in writing, include a documented business justification and compensating controls, and be approved by the BSA Officer. Exceptions are time-bound, logged in the compliance exception register, and reported to the board.

Escalation path for a suspected violation or control breakdown: line staff escalate to their manager and to Compliance; the Compliance analyst escalates to the BSA Officer; the BSA Officer escalates to the board committee and, where a violation of law is suspected, ensures a SAR is filed under MJD-CMP-0002. Suspected sanctions matches are escalated immediately under MJD-OPS-0008 and may require blocking or rejecting a transaction before any further processing.

No employee may be retaliated against for raising a BSA/AML concern in good faith. The safe-harbor protection under 31 U.S.C. 5318(g)(3) applies to good-faith SAR filings.
