# Fair Lending (ECOA / Reg B) Policy

## Purpose and Scope

This policy establishes the fair lending program for Meridian John Doe Financial (Meridian J.D.), a synthetic fintech for demonstration. It ensures that every credit decision is made without unlawful discrimination, consistent with the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) and Regulation B, and the fair lending provisions of the Fair Housing Act where applicable.

The policy applies to every stage of the credit lifecycle: marketing, application intake, underwriting, pricing, servicing, loss mitigation, and collections, across all consumer credit products. It is binding on every employee and model involved in a credit decision. It is classified CONFIDENTIAL because it exposes the institution's risk-based pricing logic and fair lending testing methodology, and it lists only the COMPLIANCE_OFFICER and RISK_ANALYST personas.

Fair lending obligations apply equally to human and automated decisioning. Models that influence credit decisions are additionally governed by the Model Risk Management Policy (MJD-RSK-0002) and are tested for disparate impact under this policy.

## Definitions

- **Prohibited basis**: Race, color, religion, national origin, sex (including sexual orientation and gender identity), marital status, age (provided the applicant has capacity to contract), receipt of public assistance income, or good-faith exercise of rights under the Consumer Credit Protection Act.
- **Disparate treatment**: Treating an applicant differently based on a prohibited basis, whether overt or through pretext.
- **Disparate impact**: A facially neutral policy or practice that has a disproportionate adverse effect on a prohibited-basis group and is not justified by business necessity.
- **Adverse action**: A denial, a revocation, an unfavorable change in terms, or a refusal to grant credit in the amount or on the terms requested.
- **Redlining**: Denying or discouraging credit in specific geographies based on the prohibited-basis composition of the area.
- **Comparative file review**: A side-by-side analysis of similarly situated approved and denied applicants to detect disparate treatment.
- **Exception**: A deviation from standard underwriting criteria or pricing that is granted to an applicant on a discretionary basis.
- **Special Purpose Credit Program (SPCP)**: A credit program designed to meet special social needs or extend credit to economically disadvantaged persons, permissible under ECOA 15 U.S.C. 1691(c)(3).

## 1. Nondiscrimination Principles

### 1.1 Core Prohibition

1.1.1 Meridian J.D. does not discriminate against any applicant on a prohibited basis in any aspect of a credit transaction. This applies to discouraging applications, the information requested, evaluation criteria, pricing, and terms.

1.1.2 The institution does not consider a prohibited basis in any credit decision except where explicitly permitted (for example, age in an empirically derived, demonstrably and statistically sound credit scoring system that treats age as a predictive variable in a non-penalizing way).

### 1.2 Marketing and Pre-Application

1.2.1 Marketing is reviewed so that it does not discourage prospective applicants on a prohibited basis and does not redline geographies. Marketing distribution is analyzed for prohibited-basis disparities.

1.2.2 Marketing channel selection, target audience criteria, and geographic distribution are documented and reviewed by the Fair Lending Officer before each campaign launch. A campaign that would result in a distribution materially skewing away from a protected-class neighborhood requires documented business justification and Fair Lending Officer approval.

### 1.3 Redlining and Geographic Analysis

1.3.1 Meridian J.D. performs geographic fair lending analysis at least annually, using census-tract-level application, origination, and denial data. The analysis maps the institution's lending activity overlaid on the racial and ethnic composition of census tracts within the institution's assessment area, as reported under the Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA) where applicable.

1.3.2 Trigger thresholds for escalation: if the denial rate in majority-minority census tracts (greater than 50% minority population) exceeds the denial rate in majority non-minority tracts by more than 10 percentage points, controlling for legitimate underwriting factors, the analysis is flagged for root-cause review. An application rate below 80% of the market penetration rate in majority-minority census tracts also triggers review.

1.3.3 The geographic analysis methodology is: (a) map all applications, approvals, denials, and withdrawals by census tract for the analysis period; (b) overlay FFIEC census demographics data; (c) compute application and denial rates by tract racial/ethnic composition band; (d) compare the institution's application penetration to that of peer institutions using HMDA aggregate data; (e) identify any census tract cluster where the institution's application rate or approval rate is meaningfully below the market, adjusted for median income.

1.3.4 Redlining findings are escalated immediately to the Fair Lending Officer and the board committee. Remediation actions may include targeted outreach programs, Community Reinvestment Act partnerships, or product access expansion. All remediation steps are documented and their effectiveness is measured in the next annual geographic analysis.

1.3.5 Branch and loan officer coverage maps are reviewed in conjunction with the geographic analysis. A gap in coverage that correlates with majority-minority census tracts is itself a fair lending concern requiring Fair Lending Officer review.

### 1.4 Special Purpose Credit Programs

1.4.1 Meridian J.D. may offer Special Purpose Credit Programs (SPCPs) that are designed to benefit a class of persons under ECOA 15 U.S.C. 1691(c)(3). An SPCP must meet all of the following conditions to be permissible: (a) it is established pursuant to a written plan that identifies the class of persons it is designed to benefit and sets forth the procedures and standards for extending credit; (b) it is designed to extend credit to a class of persons who, under the institution's customary standards, would not qualify; (c) it is a defined program, not a case-by-case exception.

1.4.2 Before launching an SPCP, the Fair Lending Officer prepares a written SPCP plan covering: the target class and the qualifying criteria (income limits, census tract eligibility, or other objective criteria); the credit terms that differ from standard; the underwriting adjustments; and the evidence that the program benefits an economically disadvantaged class. Legal reviews the plan to confirm ECOA compliance.

1.4.3 SPCPs are documented in a register maintained by the Fair Lending Officer. Each SPCP is reviewed annually to confirm it continues to serve its intended beneficiary class and to assess whether it has been applied consistently and without regard to prohibited bases within the eligible class.

1.4.4 Discrimination within an SPCP against members of the eligible class on a prohibited basis is a fair lending violation. SPCP testing is included in the annual statistical analysis described in Section 5.

## 2. Application and Underwriting Controls

### 2.1 Consistent Underwriting

2.1.1 Underwriting criteria are documented, objective, and applied consistently. Exceptions to standard criteria are logged with the reason and reviewed for disparities, because inconsistent exceptions are a common source of disparate treatment.

2.1.2 Information that may not be collected (for example, certain statements about childbearing intentions) is prohibited at intake. Monitoring information collected for regulatory purposes is kept separate from the credit decision.

### 2.2 Spousal and Co-Signer Rules

2.2.1 The institution does not require a spouse's signature where the applicant individually qualifies, consistent with Regulation B's signature rules.

### 2.3 Application Information Controls

2.3.1 Application intake forms and interviewing scripts are reviewed by the Fair Lending Officer to confirm they do not solicit prohibited information. The following categories are prohibited from solicitation or collection on the credit application: race, color, religion, national origin, sex, marital status (except for community property state disclosures and applications relying on spousal income), and receipt of public assistance income (except where income verification is a stated criterion).

2.3.2 Government monitoring information (GMI) required for HMDA reporting (race, ethnicity, sex) is collected on the separate GMI form. A firewall procedure ensures GMI data is not transmitted to underwriters or pricing staff before a credit decision is made. The technical implementation of this firewall is documented in the system design and reviewed by the COMPLIANCE_OFFICER at least annually to confirm the data remains segregated in the credit decisioning workflow.

2.3.3 Intake staff are trained that if an applicant volunteers prohibited-basis information, the staff member may not note it on the file. The response script is: acknowledge receipt of the application, redirect to the relevant qualifying information (income, assets, credit history), and note in the call record that the applicant volunteered information that cannot be used.

2.3.4 The application intake system enforces GMI firewall controls technically: GMI fields are stored in a separate table with access restricted to HMDA reporting staff. A quarterly audit by the COMPLIANCE_OFFICER confirms no unauthorized joins or exports of GMI to underwriting tables.

### 2.4 Exception Tracking Matrix

2.4.1 Every exception to standard underwriting criteria or standard pricing is captured in the exception tracking system at the time it is made. The following fields are required:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Unique exception ID | System-generated at submission |
| Application ID | Linked to origination system |
| Exception type | Underwriting override, pricing concession, program exception |
| Standard criteria deviated from | Specific guideline or rate sheet reference |
| Justification | Documented reason (compensating factors, competitive response, etc.) |
| Approver role and ID | Who approved the exception |
| Approval date | Date of approval |
| Applicant GMI available | Yes/No (used to flag for disparity monitoring; GMI value not stored here) |
| Exception outcome | Approved/Denied/Withdrawn after exception |

2.4.2 Exceptions are reviewed for prohibited-basis disparities monthly by the COMPLIANCE_OFFICER. The review compares exception grant rates and the value of pricing concessions between prohibited-basis group indicators (using GMI or surname-based proxy where GMI is unavailable for the complete file). A difference in exception grant rates of more than 5 percentage points, or a pricing concession value difference of more than 25 basis points, triggers escalation under Section 3.2.

2.4.3 Exception data is included in the quarterly fair lending management report and in the annual statistical analysis. The rolling 12-month exception grant rate and mean concession value are tracked as dashboard metrics reported to the Fair Lending Officer monthly.

## 3. Pricing and Terms

3.1 Risk-based pricing is permitted but must be driven by documented, legitimate, nondiscriminatory factors. Discretionary pricing adjustments are constrained, logged, and tested for prohibited-basis disparities. The aggregate pricing disparity tolerance that triggers escalation and root-cause analysis is a difference of 25 basis points or more in average annual percentage rate between a prohibited-basis group and the control group after controlling for legitimate factors.

### 3.2 Pricing Disparity Escalation Procedure

3.2.1 When a pricing disparity of 25 basis points or more is detected in statistical analysis or exception review, the following escalation steps apply:

1. **Detection**: COMPLIANCE_OFFICER identifies disparity in the statistical output or exception monitoring report and documents the finding with the affected product, prohibited-basis group, disparity measure, and confidence interval.
2. **Notification**: COMPLIANCE_OFFICER notifies the Fair Lending Officer within 2 business days of detection. The RISK_ANALYST is also notified to begin independent challenge.
3. **Root-cause analysis**: Fair Lending Officer convenes a root-cause working group within 5 business days. The group evaluates: (a) whether the disparity persists after adding additional legitimate control variables; (b) whether the disparity is driven by underwriting exceptions vs. pricing exceptions; (c) whether a specific loan officer, branch, or product cohort is the source.
4. **Documentation**: All root-cause analysis steps, data, and conclusions are documented in the fair lending file, which is a privileged attorney-client document. Legal is notified at Step 2 for all S1-equivalent findings (disparity above 25 bps with statistical significance at p less than 0.05).
5. **Remediation plan**: If the disparity is not fully explained by legitimate factors, the Fair Lending Officer and Legal develop a remediation plan within 15 business days of the root-cause conclusion.
6. **Regulator notification**: Legal advises on whether the disparity requires self-reporting to the prudential regulator or the Department of Justice. If so, Legal leads the notification.
7. **Follow-up monitoring**: After remediation is implemented, the statistical analysis is re-run at 90 days and 180 days to confirm the disparity has closed.

3.2.2 All escalated pricing disparity findings are tracked in the fair lending risk register (MJD-RSK-0001) to closure.

### 3.3 Discretionary Pricing Adjustment Controls

3.3.1 Loan officers and underwriters may make discretionary pricing adjustments (concessions or matches) within an approved adjustment matrix. The matrix defines the maximum adjustment by product and the required approval level:

| Adjustment amount (basis points) | Maximum adjustment per product | Approval required |
|---|---|---|
| 1 to 25 bps | Permitted per loan officer with documentation | Loan officer self-documents in exception system |
| 26 to 50 bps | Permitted per loan officer; supervisory review | Loan officer documents; branch manager reviews within 2 business days |
| 51 to 100 bps | Requires pre-approval | Branch manager pre-approves before commitment |
| Above 100 bps | Requires Fair Lending Officer review | Fair Lending Officer approves before commitment; RISK_ANALYST notified |

3.3.2 Any discretionary pricing adjustment that moves the APR below the floor specified in the product rate sheet, or that results in a total concession of more than 100 basis points for any single applicant, is automatically flagged in the exception system for Fair Lending Officer review, regardless of who approved it.

3.3.3 The 25-basis-point disparity trigger described in Section 3.1 applies at the portfolio level across similarly situated applicants within a product and time period. The individual adjustment limits in Section 3.3.1 apply at the transaction level.

## 4. Adverse Action Notices

4.1 When the institution takes adverse action, it provides the applicant a notice within 30 calendar days of receiving a completed application, stating the specific principal reasons for the action or the right to request them.

4.2 For incomplete applications, the institution either provides a notice of incompleteness or an adverse action notice within the Regulation B timeframes. Adverse action notice content and timing are validated as part of fair lending testing.

### 4.3 Appraisal and Valuation Nondiscrimination

4.3.1 Consistent with 12 CFR 1002.14, the institution promptly provides applicants copies of appraisals and other written valuations developed in connection with a credit application, no later than 3 business days before loan closing (or, in the case of adverse action, no later than the adverse action notice).

4.3.2 Appraisals and automated valuation model (AVM) outputs are monitored for prohibited-basis disparities. The COMPLIANCE_OFFICER reviews a sample of appraisals quarterly for comments or adjustments that could reflect improper prohibited-basis considerations (for example, comments referencing the racial composition of neighborhoods).

4.3.3 Reconsideration of Value (ROV) Process: any applicant who believes an appraisal contains errors, omissions, or prohibited-basis bias may submit a written ROV request. The process is:

1. Applicant submits a written ROV request with supporting documentation (comparable sales, factual corrections, etc.).
2. The originating underwriter forwards the request to the Appraisal Review function within 2 business days. The originating loan officer is excluded from the ROV process to avoid pressure on the reviewer.
3. The Appraisal Review function evaluates the request within 10 business days and documents its determination in the file.
4. If the ROV is granted, a revised appraisal or AVM output is obtained and the credit decision is re-evaluated on the revised value.
5. If the ROV is denied, the applicant is informed with reasons and the original value stands.

4.3.4 ROV requests, outcomes, and turnaround times are tracked quarterly. A pattern of ROV denials correlated with prohibited-basis characteristics of the applicant or the property's neighborhood triggers a fair lending escalation under Section 3.2.

## 5. Fair Lending Testing and Monitoring

### 5.1 Statistical Analysis

5.1.1 Compliance performs periodic statistical analysis of underwriting and pricing outcomes by prohibited-basis group, at least annually and after any material model or policy change.

5.1.2 Where statistical analysis flags a disparity, a comparative file review of similarly situated applicants is conducted to determine whether the disparity reflects disparate treatment or a legitimate factor.

### 5.2 Model Testing

5.2.1 Any model influencing credit decisions is tested for disparate impact before deployment and on the validation cadence in MJD-RSK-0002. A model showing unjustified disparate impact is remediated or withdrawn before use.

5.2.2 Proxy variables that correlate strongly with a prohibited basis without business necessity are removed.

### 5.3 Comparative File Review Procedure

5.3.1 When statistical analysis or an exception monitoring review flags a potential disparity, a comparative file review (CFR) is conducted. The CFR objective is to determine whether similarly situated applicants were treated differently based on a prohibited basis.

5.3.2 Sample selection methodology: the COMPLIANCE_OFFICER identifies a matched sample from the flagged population. Matching criteria are: same product, same time window (not more than 12 months), similar credit score band (within 20 points), similar loan-to-value ratio (within 5 percentage points), and similar debt-to-income ratio (within 5 percentage points). If the flagged population is fewer than 30 files, all files in the population are reviewed. If 30 or more, a random sample of at least 30 files per group (protected class vs. control group) is selected.

5.3.3 Each pair of files in the CFR is reviewed using a standardized template that documents: identifying underwriting factors, pricing factors, exception history, adverse action reasons (if applicable), and any notation in the file that refers (directly or through a proxy) to a prohibited basis. The reviewer is a COMPLIANCE_OFFICER who was not involved in the origination of the file.

5.3.4 CFR findings are documented in a privilege-protected memo shared with Legal. If the CFR identifies specific files where treatment differences cannot be explained by legitimate factors, those files are referred to Legal for individual remediation consideration.

### 5.4 Disparate Impact Testing Methodology

5.4.1 Disparate impact testing uses a regression-based approach. The analysis controls for the following legitimate underwriting and pricing variables: credit score (using the score type used in origination), loan-to-value ratio, debt-to-income ratio, loan amount, loan purpose (purchase vs. refinance), property type, loan term, and geographic market (Metropolitan Statistical Area). After controlling for these variables, the residual difference in approval rate or pricing attributable to prohibited-basis group membership is the disparate impact measure.

5.4.2 Threshold for escalation: a residual disparity that is statistically significant at the 95% confidence level (p less than 0.05) and has a practical effect size of more than 5 percentage points in approval rates, or more than 25 basis points in pricing, triggers the escalation procedure in Section 3.2.

5.4.3 The RISK_ANALYST independently replicates the regression with alternative model specifications to test whether the result is robust to specification choice. If the RISK_ANALYST's independent analysis reaches a materially different conclusion, the Fair Lending Officer convenes a reconciliation meeting within 5 business days.

5.4.4 Disparate impact analysis is performed annually on the full prior-year origination population and on any model change that affects more than 10% of the origination volume. Results are included in the annual fair lending report to the board.

## 6. Record-Keeping Requirements

6.1 Regulation B (12 CFR 1002.12) requires retention of records related to credit applications for 25 months from the date of the adverse action notice, the notification of action taken, or the date the application was received if no action was taken. Meridian J.D. retains all records described in this section for the 25-month minimum or, where another law requires longer retention, for the longer period. Retention schedules are maintained in MJD-CMP-0008.

6.2 Records retained per Regulation B and this policy:

| Record type | Retention period | Trigger | System of record |

| Credit application (all fields) | 25 months | Date of adverse action notice or notification of action taken | Origination LOS |
| Adverse action notices | 25 months | Date of notice | Origination LOS |
| Appraisals and valuations | 25 months | Date of adverse action or closing | Document management system |

6.3 All records are stored in approved systems of record with access restricted per MJD-SEC-0003. Records containing NPI are protected per MJD-CMP-0005.

6.4 The COMPLIANCE_OFFICER conducts a quarterly spot-check of the origination LOS to confirm that required records are present and complete for a sample of applications. Gaps are reported to the Fair Lending Officer within 5 business days of discovery.

## 7. Remediation Procedures

7.1 When a fair lending violation is identified, the Fair Lending Officer and Legal determine whether consumer remediation is required. Consumer remediation is required when a specific consumer received less favorable terms or was improperly denied credit as a result of the violation.

7.2 Consumer remediation calculation: for pricing violations, the remediation amount is the difference between the rate or fee actually charged and the rate or fee the consumer would have received absent the violation, applied over the life of the loan to the point of remediation, and expressed as a restitution payment. For denial violations, remediation includes re-offering the credit on the terms for which the consumer qualified, and compensating the consumer for documented consequential damages up to a ceiling approved by Legal.

7.3 Remediation steps:

1. Fair Lending Officer and Legal identify the affected consumer population and calculate the remediation amount per consumer.
2. Finance Controller confirms the total remediation liability and sources funds.
3. A notification letter is prepared by Legal, reviewed by the Chief Privacy Officer for NPI compliance, and approved by the Fair Lending Officer. The letter: acknowledges the error, states the remediation amount and form of payment, and provides a contact for questions. The letter does not admit unlawful discrimination unless Legal expressly directs that disclosure.
4. Remediation payments are issued within 60 days of the population identification. Wire or check per consumer election.
5. Consumers who cannot be located after good-faith outreach (return mail, no active account) are handled per the unclaimed property process.
6. Where regulator reporting is required, the regulatory submission is coordinated by Legal concurrent with consumer notification.

7.4 Remediation records are retained for 7 years from the date of remediation, consistent with MJD-CMP-0008.

## Roles and Responsibilities

- **Fair Lending Officer**: Owns this policy and the testing program; leads remediation; reports to the board committee; escalation time-to-respond is 2 business days for High findings, 5 business days for Medium findings.
- **Compliance Officer (COMPLIANCE_OFFICER persona)**: Conducts statistical analysis, comparative file reviews, exception monitoring, and adverse action notice validation; escalation time-to-respond is 1 business day for detection of a 25-bps pricing disparity.
- **Risk Analyst (RISK_ANALYST persona)**: Provides independent challenge of pricing and model fairness; integrates fair lending risk into MJD-RSK-0001; escalation time-to-respond is 3 business days for independent replication of statistical findings.
- **Underwriting and Lending Staff**: Apply criteria consistently; document exceptions at the time they are made; do not solicit prohibited information.
- **Legal**: Provides privilege protection for fair lending analysis; advises on regulator notification; drafts remediation letters.
- **Finance Controller**: Funds and processes consumer remediation payments.

## Exceptions and Escalation

Underwriting or pricing exceptions are logged with documented justification and reviewed for disparity. A confirmed disparate-treatment finding or an unjustified disparate-impact finding is escalated immediately to the Fair Lending Officer and the board committee, and remediation, including potential consumer remediation, is tracked to closure. A pricing disparity of 25 basis points or more after controlling for legitimate factors triggers mandatory root-cause analysis. Suspected discrimination by an employee is escalated to the Fair Lending Officer and Human Resources.
