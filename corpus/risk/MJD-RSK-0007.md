# Fraud Risk Management Procedure

## Purpose and Scope

This Fraud Risk Management Procedure defines how Meridian John Doe Financial ("Meridian J.D." or the "Bank") prevents, detects, investigates, and responds to fraud across the enterprise. It implements the fraud dimension of the Enterprise Risk Management Framework (MJD-RSK-0001) and the Operational Risk Procedure (MJD-RSK-0003), and provides the concrete workflows, thresholds, and timelines that fraud, operations, security, and compliance staff follow.

Fraud risk is the risk of an intentional act of deception that results in financial, operational, or reputational harm to the Bank or its customers. The Procedure covers internal fraud (committed by staff), external fraud (committed by customers or third parties such as account takeover, payment fraud, check fraud, and application fraud), and collusive fraud. It binds the fraud risk function, operations analysts who triage alerts and execute first-line controls, security architects who own the technical detection and identity controls, and compliance officers who own the regulatory reporting interface.

The Procedure applies across all channels and products, including branch, online, mobile, card, wire, ACH, and lending.

## Definitions

**Fraud event.** A confirmed or suspected intentional act of deception causing or risking loss.

**Account takeover (ATO).** Unauthorized access to and control of a legitimate customer account.

**First-party fraud.** Fraud committed by the legitimate account holder, such as application fraud or bust-out.

**Mule account.** An account used to receive and move the proceeds of fraud.

**Fraud alert.** An automated or manual signal indicating possible fraud, generated by the detection systems.

**Confirmed fraud loss.** A loss established as fraud after investigation, net of recoveries.

**Suspicious Activity Report (SAR).** The regulatory report filed when activity meets the suspicious activity reporting criteria.

## 1. Fraud Governance

The Head of Fraud Risk owns this Procedure and reports to the Operational Risk Committee and through the CRO. A cross-functional Fraud Risk Working Group, including fraud, operations, security, and compliance, meets monthly to review fraud trends, losses, emerging schemes, and control effectiveness. Fraud detection models are governed as models under the Model Risk Management Policy (MJD-RSK-0002).

## 2. Fraud Prevention Controls

### 2.1 Customer authentication

Customer authentication uses risk-based, multi-factor controls. High-risk actions, such as adding a payee, changing contact details, or initiating a wire, require step-up authentication. The technical authentication and identity controls are owned by security and implemented per the Information Security Policy (MJD-SEC-0001).

### 2.2 Transaction controls

Payment channels apply velocity limits, new-payee holds, and out-of-band confirmation for high-value transfers. Wire and high-value payment controls are coordinated with operations. Dual approval applies to transfers above defined thresholds consistent with operations dual-approval controls.

### 2.3 Internal fraud controls

Segregation of duties, mandatory leave for sensitive roles, and privileged access monitoring reduce internal fraud. Privileged access is governed by security policy and monitored through logging and SIEM controls owned by security.

## 3. Fraud Detection

### 3.1 Detection signals

Fraud is detected through real-time transaction scoring, rules, anomaly detection, customer reports, and staff referrals. Detection models produce a fraud score; transactions above a score threshold generate an alert or are held pending review.

### 3.2 Alert triage thresholds and timing

Alerts are queued by severity and risk score. Triage service levels are:

| Alert severity | Triggering condition | First-touch SLA |
|---|---|---|
| Critical | Confirmed or high-probability ATO; wire above USD 50,000 flagged | 15 minutes |
| High | Card or ACH alert above USD 10,000; multiple-rule hit | 1 hour |
| Medium | Single-rule hit USD 1,000 to USD 10,000 | 4 business hours |
| Low | Score below action threshold, monitoring only | 1 business day |

Operations analysts perform first-line triage. A Critical alert that cannot be cleared within the SLA is escalated to the fraud investigations team immediately.

## 4. Fraud Investigation

### 4.1 Workflow

1. The investigator opens a case linked to the alert and the affected accounts.
2. Funds at risk are held or recalled where possible; for unauthorized electronic fund transfers, provisional credit and error resolution follow the Regulation E Error Resolution Procedure (MJD-CMP-0006).
3. The investigator gathers evidence, confirms or clears the fraud, and quantifies gross and net loss.
4. Confirmed fraud is recorded as an operational loss event per the Operational Risk Procedure (MJD-RSK-0003).
5. The case is referred to compliance for suspicious activity reporting assessment where the criteria may be met.

### 4.2 Recovery

The investigator pursues recovery through payment network recall, holds on receiving mule accounts, and law enforcement referral for losses above USD 25,000. Recoveries are netted against the recorded loss.

## 5. Regulatory Reporting Interface

Where investigation findings indicate activity that meets suspicious activity reporting criteria, the case is referred to compliance, which owns the SAR filing under the relevant BSA/AML procedures. Fraud staff provide the underlying facts; compliance determines and executes the filing within the regulatory timeline. Fraud and compliance reconcile referred cases monthly so no reportable matter is missed.

## 6. Fraud Scheme Playbooks

Each major scheme has a playbook specifying the detection signals, the immediate first-line containment, the investigation focus, and the recovery levers. The playbooks below are the most material schemes; each is reviewed by the Fraud Risk Working Group at least annually.

### 6.1 Account takeover playbook

1. Detection signals: anomalous login geography or device, password and contact-detail change followed by a new payee, and a rapid high-value transfer attempt.
2. Immediate containment: lock outbound payments, suspend the changed credentials, and place a hold on any pending transfer above USD 1,000.
3. Investigation focus: confirm the genuine customer through out-of-band verification, identify the entry vector, and check for related compromised accounts on the same device fingerprint.
4. Recovery: recall in-flight payments, freeze receiving mule accounts, and reissue credentials. Reset step-up authentication per the Information Security Policy (MJD-SEC-0001).

### 6.2 Authorized push payment and social-engineering scam playbook

1. Detection signals: a first-time payee, a payment narrative consistent with invoice or romance or investment fraud, and customer hesitation flagged at the branch or call center.
2. Immediate containment: invoke a confirmation-of-payee check and a cooling-off hold on first-time high-value payees.
3. Investigation focus: determine whether the customer was deceived into authorizing the payment, since liability and Regulation E treatment differ from unauthorized transfers.
4. Recovery: attempt beneficiary-bank recall within the network rules and report the receiving account.

### 6.3 Application and first-party fraud playbook

1. Detection signals: synthetic-identity indicators, velocity of applications from one device, and early-payment-default patterns indicating bust-out intent.
2. Immediate containment: decline or hold funding, reduce limits, and flag linked applications.
3. Investigation focus: verify identity elements against authoritative sources and link to known fraud rings.
4. Recovery: charge off per the Credit Risk Policy (MJD-RSK-0004) and refer for prosecution where loss exceeds the threshold in Section 4.2.

### 6.4 Internal fraud playbook

1. Detection signals: segregation-of-duties conflicts, privileged-access anomalies, suppressed alerts, and reluctance to take mandatory leave.
2. Immediate containment: suspend access discreetly and preserve evidence, coordinating with security and human resources.
3. Investigation focus: reconstruct the activity from immutable logs owned by security; quantify loss and exposure window.
4. Recovery: pursue restitution, file required reports through compliance, and remediate the control gap.

## 7. Fraud Loss and Performance Metrics

The Bank tracks fraud performance through a fixed metric set reported monthly to the Fraud Risk Working Group. A red metric is escalated under Section Exceptions and Escalation.

| Metric | Definition | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fraud loss rate (cards) | Net fraud loss / card spend (bps) | <= 5 bps | 5 to 8 bps | > 8 bps |
| Detection rate | Value blocked / total attempted fraud | >= 85% | 75% to 85% | < 75% |
| False-positive ratio | Genuine declined / total declined | <= 8:1 | 8:1 to 12:1 | > 12:1 |
| Recovery rate | Recovered / gross confirmed loss | >= 35% | 25% to 35% | < 25% |
| Critical alert SLA adherence | Critical alerts triaged within SLA | >= 98% | 95% to 98% | < 95% |

A sustained rise in the false-positive ratio is treated as a model performance signal and refers the detection model for review under the Model Risk Management Policy (MJD-RSK-0002).

## Exceptions and Escalation

Any deviation from this Procedure, such as a triage SLA waiver or a control bypass for a high-value payment, requires documented approval by the Head of Fraud Risk with a compensating control and an expiry not exceeding 90 days. Bypassing dual approval on high-value transfers may not be granted by standing exception.

Escalation: a confirmed fraud loss at or above USD 1 million, a coordinated fraud event affecting many customers, or a suspected internal fraud is escalated to the Head of Fraud Risk within one hour and to the CRO within 24 hours under the Enterprise Risk Management Framework (MJD-RSK-0001). Suspected internal fraud involving privileged access is escalated in parallel to security under the Information Security Policy (MJD-SEC-0001). Matters meeting suspicious activity criteria are escalated to compliance immediately.
